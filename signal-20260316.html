Every yield maturity is 2.7–2.9 standard deviations above its 20-day mean. And the loudest signal today isn't oil or gold: it's the grain complex, Libya, and a dollar that's stopped benefiting from its own yields.

Read that chart from bottom to top. Bitcoin is 1.62 standard deviations below its 20-day mean — the market prices it as a risk asset in a fear environment. The oil market is the most dislocated instrument in the universe today. Not gold. Not VIX. Oil.

The Dollar Isn't Following Its Own Yields

Mortgage rate 30Y: 6.11% (reversed upward — real economy tightening)

Here is the structural signal. Three weeks ago, yields were rising and so was the dollar — that's the standard playbook: higher US rates attract capital, dollar strengthens. That relationship has inverted. Yields are now 2.7–2.9σ above their 20-day mean across every maturity. But the dollar z-score is 0.19. Capital is not flowing into the US despite the yield offer.

The mechanism: bonds are selling off for the wrong reason. When yields rise because growth is strong, the dollar follows — investors buy US assets. When yields rise because supply is abundant, fiscal trajectory is uncertain, or risk premium is expanding, it's the opposite — capital wants out of US duration, not in. The term premium reversing upward to 0.549% is the market explicitly saying: "I want extra return for holding this risk, not just for giving up liquidity." That is not a growth premium. That is a risk premium. And risk premiums on US Treasuries don't attract dollar buyers — they repel them.

Yield Curve — Sigma Above 20-Day Mean (All Maturities)

The entire yield curve is in simultaneous breakout. This is not a steepening story or a short-end-only story — every maturity from 2 to 30 years is 2.7σ or more above its 20-day mean. Credit spreads confirm: IG OAS at 0.93 is 2.4σ above its 20-day mean; HY OAS at 3.28 is 2.2σ above. Both reversed upward this week after a brief pullback. The HY-IG spread sits at 2.26 — credit markets are pricing stress, not panic, but the direction is wrong. The 2-10 spread at +0.51% and the 10-30 at +0.61% are consistent with a bear steepener: the long end selling faster than the short end, which is a stagflation signal, not a growth one. The bear steepener, high term premium, rising credit spreads, and the dollar's failure to benefit from its own yields are telling the same story: this is not confidence-driven. This is risk-premium-driven. The bond market is not saying the US economy is hot. It is saying US assets require a higher premium to hold.

Gold at 5,019 — Below January's Peak Despite a War

Gold closed at 5,019 today, a 0.6% pullback from last week's close of 5,062 (March 13). The gold z-score is 1.77 — elevated but not extreme. Here is the fact that deserves attention: gold's intraday high on January 29 was $5,595. Today, with a hot war, a de facto Hormuz closure, and oil above $100, gold is at 5,019. That is $576 — or 10% — below the January high.

Two readings are possible. The first: January was a speculative overshoot driven by geopolitical expectations, and the actual war has produced a more measured repricing because the market already priced a scenario this severe. The second: the gold bid is structurally constrained by dollar strength — DXY above 100 creates a real headwind to gold priced in dollars, and the two are competing. Both are probably partially true. But the practical takeaway is this: gold has not confirmed a new structural high. It has reclaimed the $5,000 psychological level and is holding it. That is a necessary condition for the next leg — but not sufficient. The GLD ETF holds 744.9 tonnes. Holdings are stable, not surging. This is not a panic bid.

Oil at 5.2 Sigma — And Libya Is Burning Silently

FIRMS Fire Detection — Geopolitical Zones (16 March 2026)
Libya: The Secondary Story Nobody's Pricing

Libya is registering 823 fire detections today, including 70 high-confidence readings — more than three times the activity in the Strait of Hormuz and nearly as high as Saudi Arabia. Libya produces approximately 1.2 million barrels per day and has long been vulnerable to internal disruption. The high-confidence count of 70 is significant: these are thermal anomalies consistent with oil infrastructure, not weather or agriculture. The market is focused on Hormuz. The data says there may be a secondary supply disruption developing in North Africa that hasn't been priced.

The Israel-Lebanon front has essentially gone quiet — 28 detections, zero high-confidence. That front de-escalated as the Iran conflict escalated. The war's focus has shifted entirely to the Persian Gulf and Iranian interior, with satellite data showing Iran at 1,359 detections (41 high-confidence), consistent with sustained military operations. The Strait of Hormuz itself shows 222 detections — constant activity in the strait zone, consistent with continued naval interdiction operations and attempted transits.

The selective passage arrangement is the real Hormuz story right now. Iran has granted safe passage to Indian and Turkish vessels. China is in negotiations for crude oil and Qatari LNG access. This is not a blockade — it is a geopolitical sorting mechanism. The strait is closed to the US and its allies, and selectively open to nations that Iran sees as potential leverage points. The practical effect: a new benchmark emerges for "non-sanctioned" oil, and the Brent-WTI spread (currently just $0.50) may widen again as the geographic tiering becomes more explicit.

Corn and wheat both exceed the 3% single-session threshold for significant grain moves today. The mechanism is the Hormuz-fertilizer supply chain: enormous volumes of urea and ammonia — the primary nitrogen fertilizers — transit the strait from Qatar and Iran. A prolonged Hormuz closure tightens fertilizer supply with a 90-to-180-day lag, and that lag is now closing. Farmers in the US Midwest are accelerating sales as prices rise. Export demand is picking up: South Korea and Taiwan both tendered for US wheat this week.

The World Bank's February Food Price Index rose 2.1% — the first significant upward move after a year of stability. The cereal component rose 1.1%, with wheat up 1.8% driven partly by frost damage in the US and Europe. Today's moves layer on top of that baseline. The practical consequence: the March CPI print, currently expected around April 10, will show energy and food components both elevated simultaneously. The last time that happened at this scale was 2022. The Fed, which meets this week, cannot ignore this input — but it also cannot do anything about it.

FOMC This Week: Powell's Penultimate Meeting

Jerome Powell chairs his second-to-last FOMC meeting this week. His term expires May 15. The market expects a hold at 3.75% — priced at near-certainty. The dots are what matters. Going into this meeting: oil above $100, grain prices surging, all yield maturities 2.7-2.9σ above their 20-day means, credit spreads widening, and the 2-year Treasury at 3.76% (2.9σ above mean). The sum of those inputs is not a meeting that produces dovish signals.

The market has already pushed the first full cut to September 2026 — just one cut priced for the full year. The FOMC's challenge is that the inflation signals are supply-shock-driven: energy and food. The Fed cannot lower energy prices by raising rates. But it also cannot cut while supply-driven inflation is accelerating. The honest answer is "wait and see," which is exactly what a hold communicates. Expect Powell to acknowledge energy price pressures explicitly, note the uncertainty around the geopolitical trajectory, and signal no change to the current trajectory.

The word-count bet on Polymarket (43.5% for "inflation" 50+ times) captures the market's expectation correctly. This is a hawkish-toned hold. The new Fed chair's identity remains the wildcard for June onward.
